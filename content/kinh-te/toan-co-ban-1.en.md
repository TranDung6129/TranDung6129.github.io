From an economics perspective, the derivative is not just a tool for calculating rates of change but also the foundation for the concept of **marginality**, a core concept in microeconomics and macroeconomics.

## 1. Concept of Marginal Functions

In economics, when we consider a variable $y$ depending on $x$, the derivative $\frac{dy}{dx}$ tells us the change in $y$ when $x$ changes by a very small unit. We call this the concept of **marginality**.

For example, the cost function $C(q)$ represents the total cost to produce $q$ products. In this case, **Marginal Cost (MC)** is the derivative of the cost function:

$$ MC(q) = C'(q) = \frac{dC}{dq} $$

Economic meaning: If we are producing $q$ products, the cost to produce one more product is approximately $MC(q)$.

## 2. Application of Derivatives in Consumption Functions

The consumption function often takes the form:
$$ C = f(Y) $$
Where $C$ is the level of consumption and $Y$ is national income.

**Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC)** is defined as the rate of change of consumption with respect to income:
$$ MPC = \frac{dC}{dY} $$

Usually, $0 < MPC < 1$, meaning that when income increases by one unit, the public will consume a little less than one unit (and save the rest).

Derivatives help us not only model trends but also optimize economic policies based on market responses.
